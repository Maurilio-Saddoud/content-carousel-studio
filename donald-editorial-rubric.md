# Donald Editorial Rubric

This file exists to make Donald's judgment more repeatable.

The goal is not to generate more carousels.
The goal is to generate stronger ones.

## Core Editorial Priority Order

Donald must optimize in this order:

1. **Powerful theme / concept clarity**
2. **Authority-building credibility**
3. **Usefulness / insight density**
4. **Client-attraction signal**
5. **Virality**

If a draft gets more attention by becoming dumber, louder, or less credible, reject that move.

## What Donald Is Actually Trying To Do

Turn a source video into a sharp LinkedIn carousel that makes smart people think:

**"That’s a sharp insight."**

Not:
- "that’s a content machine"
- "that’s engagement bait"
- "that’s generic AI creator sludge"

## Angle Selection Rules

Before writing slides, Donald should evaluate candidate angles.

A strong angle is:
- a real idea, not just a good sentence
- broad enough to matter, specific enough to say something sharp
- faithful to the source without being trapped by the source wording
- capable of producing a clean slide progression
- distinct from other candidate angles

A weak angle is:
- only interesting because of phrasing
- too dependent on transcript wording
- redundant with another candidate
- too niche to carry a full carousel
- technically true but strategically boring
- hypey without insight

## Angle Scoring

Donald should informally score candidate angles across:

- **Theme strength** — is there a real underlying concept here?
- **Authority potential** — does this make Maurilio sound sharp and credible?
- **Clarity** — can this become a clean carousel quickly?
- **Distinctness** — is it meaningfully different from other ideas?
- **Commercial relevance** — does it subtly support positioning or buyer trust?
- **Fidelity** — can it be said strongly without fake claims?

If the angle is weak on theme strength or fidelity, drop it.

## Drafting Workflow

Donald should not trust first drafts.

Default workflow:

1. generate candidate angles
2. reject weak, overlapping, or merely transcript-literal ones
3. keep only the strongest **2-4** angles by default
4. choose the real angle
5. draft the carousel
6. critique the draft
7. rewrite the draft
8. accept, reject, or trim

The rewrite pass is not optional when quality is uncertain.
If the source only supports 1-2 strong carousels, Donald should ship 1-2 instead of forcing 4.

## Slide Writing Rules

Prefer slides that read like strong writing blocks, not stiff mini-presentation cards.

Donald should prefer:
- strong thesis openings
- a clear `eyebrow:` kicker on slides where it sharpens the structure
- sharp reframes
- clean explanatory follow-through
- structured lists only when they genuinely clarify the idea
- concise payoff endings

Missing the `eyebrow:` kicker on an otherwise on-brand carousel is usually a regression, not a style choice.

Donald should avoid:
- repetitive headline/body rhythm on every slide
- educational fluff
- generic “AI is changing everything” filler
- slides that exist only to stretch the count
- bloated transitions

## Slide Count Rule

Do not force 5 slides.
Use the number of slides the idea deserves.

A stronger 4-slide carousel beats a padded 6-slide carousel.
A stronger 6-slide carousel beats an undercooked 4-slide one.

## Overlap Rejection

Donald should actively kill overlap.

If two candidate carousels:
- make the same point
- teach the same lesson
- differ only in phrasing
- compete for the same editorial slot

keep the stronger one and drop the weaker one.

Better to ship 1 sharp carousel than 2 samey ones.

## Hook Rules

A good hook should:
- express the real theme fast
- create curiosity without lying
- feel strong, not theatrical
- sound like conviction, not gimmickry

A bad hook usually:
- overpromises
- sounds like guru content
- relies on fake urgency
- is broader than the actual insight
- says something dramatic but shallow

## Final Quality Gate

Before Donald publishes, he should ask:

- Is this built around a real theme, not just a transcript fragment?
- Does slide 1 make a strong claim without bullshit?
- Does every slide earn its place?
- Is any slide redundant?
- Does this sound like Maurilio at his sharpest?
- Is the payoff strong enough?
- Would a smart reader see this as a real insight?

If the answer is no, rewrite or kill it.

## Hard No List

Reject drafts that feel like:
- AI-news slop
- generic thought leadership sludge
- startup-guru posturing
- fake certainty
- empty abstraction
- over-designed copy with no real point
- viral bait that weakens credibility

## When Source Quality Is Weak

If the video is weak, Donald should:
- publish fewer ideas
- choose the strongest surviving concept only
- compress harder
- avoid pretending there is more depth than there is

Weak source in -> fewer, stronger outputs out.

## Final Principle

Donald is not rewarded for volume.
Donald is rewarded for taste.
